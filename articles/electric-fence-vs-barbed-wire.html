Electric Fence vs Barbed Wire: Which Is Right for Your Property?

February 14, 2025 6 min read BESITS Team
Back to Blog

When it comes to protecting the perimeter of a home, commercial property, or farm in Ghana, two options dominate the market: the traditional barbed wire fence and the increasingly popular electric fence. Both have their place, but they are not interchangeable. The right choice depends on your security goals, budget, property type, and your obligations under Ghanaian law. This article breaks down both options honestly so you can make an informed decision.

Overview

Barbed Wire

Barbed wire has been used in Ghana for decades as a low-cost perimeter barrier. It is typically stretched across a wall top or mounted on steel poles and relies entirely on physical discomfort to deter intruders. An experienced burglar with thick gloves, a pair of wire cutters, or even a thick piece of cloth can defeat most barbed wire installations within seconds. Its primary value today is as a visual signal that a boundary exists, and as a minor obstacle for opportunistic trespassers.

Electric Fencing

Modern electric fence systems use a high-voltage, low-amperage energiser to deliver a sharp, non-lethal shock to anyone who touches the electrified wires. The shock is safe but deeply unpleasant — typically 8,000 to 10,000 volts at a fraction of an amp. Critically, most modern systems also include an alarm component: if a wire is cut or tampered with, the energiser triggers an alert to a control panel, a siren, or your smartphone. This converts the fence from a passive barrier into an active alarm system.

Upfront Cost

Barbed wire remains cheaper to install on day one. A basic barbed wire installation for an average residential plot in Greater Accra might cost GHS 800–2,500 depending on perimeter length and pole spacing. Electric fence systems carry a higher initial investment, typically GHS 3,000–8,000+ for a residential property, driven by the cost of the energiser unit, mounting brackets, insulators, and professional installation.

However, the cost comparison over five years tells a different story — which brings us to maintenance.

Deterrence Level

Electric fencing wins this category decisively. Professional burglars know how to defeat barbed wire quickly and quietly. An electric fence is significantly harder to bypass: cutting a wire triggers an alarm, attempting to climb through delivers a painful shock, and the visible warning signs alone deter the majority of casual intruders before they even approach. For high-value properties, businesses, warehouses, and compounds in areas with elevated security risk, electric fencing provides a level of deterrence that barbed wire simply cannot match.

Barbed wire still has value as a physical boundary marker for farms, agricultural land, and low-risk residential plots where the goal is primarily to mark the perimeter and deter casual trespassers rather than motivated criminals.

Maintenance

Barbed wire requires surprisingly frequent maintenance in the Ghanaian climate. Moisture and humidity cause rapid rusting, wire sags over time, and vegetation growing up against the fence can create bypass opportunities. A barbed wire installation that looks acceptable on day one may need partial replacement within three to four years.

Electric fences are more robust in the long run. The energiser units used by BESITS are designed for tropical climates and typically carry five-year warranties. Annual maintenance checks — inspecting wire tension, testing voltage output, and clearing vegetation near the wires — are recommended but minimal. Quality components installed correctly can serve effectively for ten or more years.

Legal Considerations in Ghana

This is an area where many property owners in Ghana are uninformed. There is no blanket law in Ghana prohibiting electric fences, but there are important obligations:

  • Warning signs are mandatory. Every electric fence must carry clearly visible warning signs at regular intervals, particularly near gates and public access points. Failure to display warnings creates serious liability if someone is injured.
  • Safe voltage standards apply. A properly installed system must operate within safe parameters — high voltage but low amperage. Systems that exceed safe thresholds expose the property owner to legal and civil liability.
  • Permit requirements vary by local authority. Some district assemblies require a permit for electric fence installation. Your installer should confirm local requirements before commencing work.
  • Barbed wire near public walkways can also attract liability if positioned in a way that poses danger to passing pedestrians or children.

Our Recommendation

For any residential property, business premises, or compound in an urban or peri-urban area of Ghana — including communities like Afienya, Tema, Ashaiman, and Accra — we recommend electric fencing without reservation. The combination of active deterrence, tamper alarms, and long-term durability makes it the superior choice for properties where security genuinely matters.

Barbed wire remains a cost-effective option for marking boundaries on agricultural land, rural plots, or very low-risk perimeters where the primary goal is simply defining the boundary rather than stopping a determined intruder.

In many installations, BESITS combines both: an electric fence system on the primary perimeter, with barbed wire used on secondary or agricultural boundaries. This layered approach maximises security where it matters most while managing costs effectively across larger properties.
